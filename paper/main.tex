\begin{abstract}
Parameter-efficient fine-tuning with Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) has become
the standard mechanism for specializing large pretrained models, and
federated learning (FL) promises to extend it to settings where data cannot
leave the organizations that own it. Yet real cross-silo collaborations
violate three assumptions made by existing federated LoRA systems
simultaneously: participants operate in \emph{different domains} (statistical
heterogeneity at the level of entire data distributions, not merely label
skew), they possess \emph{vastly different computational resources} (system
heterogeneity spanning orders of magnitude), and they are often unable or
unwilling to rely on a \emph{central coordinating server} (decentralization).
We present \textbf{AH-LoRA}, a framework for serverless, peer-to-peer
federated fine-tuning in which every client trains a LoRA adapter whose rank
is matched to both its resource budget and the intrinsic complexity of its
local data. The technical core is a \emph{gauge-invariant aggregation
operator} that merges adapters of different ranks in the space of effective
weight updates $\dW = (\alpha/r)\,BA$ and re-factorizes the result to each
client's own rank by truncated SVD, making heterogeneous and even
\emph{time-varying} ranks first-class citizens of gossip-style
communication. On top of this primitive, AH-LoRA (i) discovers latent domain
structure by clustering adapter signatures---without sharing data or domain
labels, (ii) organizes communication hierarchically, with frequent
within-domain gossip and sparse cross-domain exchange governed by
\emph{learned transfer weights} that quantify how much each domain should
influence every other, and (iii) adapts ranks during training in response to
convergence and resource signals. We establish that aggregation in
update space is the enabling primitive---factor-space averaging is provably
ill-posed under the LoRA gauge ambiguity and rank heterogeneity---and analyze
the convergence of the resulting hierarchical, transfer-weighted gossip.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Large pretrained models are rarely deployed as-is; they are adapted. Low-Rank
Adaptation (LoRA)~\citep{hu2022lora} has emerged as the dominant adaptation
mechanism because it confines learning to a pair of small matrices per layer,
$B \in \R^{d_{\mathrm{out}} \times r}$ and $A \in \R^{r \times
d_{\mathrm{in}}}$, leaving the pretrained weights frozen. The same property
that makes LoRA cheap to train---its compactness---also makes it cheap to
\emph{communicate}, which has made it the natural unit of exchange for
federated fine-tuning: organizations that cannot pool their data can instead
exchange adapters~\citep{zhang2023fedit, babakniya2023slora}.

The promise is compelling, but the assumptions baked into current federated
LoRA systems do not survive contact with real cross-silo collaborations.
Consider a consortium of hospitals, banks, and research laboratories that
wish to co-train a shared foundation model. Three frictions arise at once.

\textbf{(C1) Domain heterogeneity.} Participants do not merely hold
differently-skewed samples of one distribution; they operate in
\emph{different domains}. A cardiology unit, an insurance claims processor,
and a physics laboratory induce weight updates that point in genuinely
different directions. Averaging such updates uniformly---the implicit policy
of FedAvg~\citep{mcmahan2017fedavg} and its LoRA descendants---dilutes every
domain's expertise to serve a global model that is optimal for none of
them. The right behavior is selective: aggregate aggressively among peers
solving the same problem, and transfer \emph{deliberately and weightedly}
across domains, importing only what helps. Crucially, in practice nobody
hands the system a domain partition: \emph{domain structure must be
discovered}, and discovered without inspecting raw data.

\textbf{(C2) System heterogeneity.} The hospital may have a hundred GPUs;
the rural clinic, one. A single global LoRA rank either excludes weak
participants (rank too high) or wastes the capacity of strong ones (rank too
low). Rank should instead be chosen \emph{per client}---high enough to
capture the complexity of the local domain, low enough to respect the local
compute, memory, and bandwidth budget---and should be allowed to \emph{change}
as training progresses and resources fluctuate. But heterogeneous ranks break
the arithmetic of federated averaging: matrices of different shapes cannot be
summed, and existing remedies (zero-padding~\citep{cho2024hetlora},
server-side SVD redistribution~\citep{bai2024flexlora}) presuppose exactly
the centralized coordinator we wish to remove.

\textbf{(C3) Decentralization.} A central aggregation server is a single
point of failure, a privacy chokepoint that observes every participant's
updates, and---in regulated industries---often a legal impossibility.
Gossip-based decentralized learning~\citep{lian2017dpsgd,
koloskova2020unified} removes the server: clients exchange models only with
graph neighbors. Yet the gossip literature almost universally assumes
\emph{homogeneous} models mixed with \emph{domain-blind} weights, precisely
the assumptions that C1 and C2 invalidate.

Each pairwise combination of these challenges has been studied: federated
LoRA with heterogeneous ranks but a central server~\citep{cho2024hetlora,
bai2024flexlora}; clustered FL that respects domain structure but relies on
the server to perform the clustering~\citep{ghosh2020ifca, sattler2020cfl};
decentralized gossip with convergence guarantees but identical, domain-blind
models~\citep{lian2017dpsgd, koloskova2020unified}. To our knowledge,
\emph{no existing system addresses the intersection}: serverless training of
heterogeneous-rank adapters across latent, undeclared domains. This paper
targets exactly that intersection.

\paragraph{Why the intersection is more than the sum of its parts.}
The obstruction is not merely architectural but algebraic. The LoRA
factorization is not unique: for any invertible $U \in \R^{r \times r}$, the
pairs $(B, A)$ and $(BU, U^{-1}A)$ encode \emph{the same} weight update
$BA$. Two clients may therefore have learned nearly identical updates whose
factor matrices look nothing alike, and entry-wise averaging of factors---the
operation at the heart of every FedAvg-style scheme---can destroy the very
information it is meant to combine. With heterogeneous ranks the factors do
not even have compatible shapes. And in a peer-to-peer setting there is no
server at which a clever one-off repair could be centralized: every client
must be able to merge arbitrary-rank neighbors, every round, locally. Our
resolution is to perform \emph{all} aggregation in the space of effective
updates $\dW_i = (\alpha_i / r_i) B_i A_i$---a representation that is
invariant to the factorization ambiguity, dimension-compatible across ranks
by construction, and absorbs each client's scaling $\alpha_i/r_i$---and to
return to each client's own rank afterwards by truncated SVD. This single
primitive (\S\ref{sec:method-agg}) is what allows rank heterogeneity (C2),
domain-selective mixing (C1), and serverless operation (C3) to coexist, and
it is cheap: the SVD is applied to matrices whose rank is bounded by the sum
of a node's neighborhood ranks, not by layer dimensions.

\paragraph{Our approach.}
We present \textbf{AH-LoRA} (Adaptive Heterogeneous LoRA), a decentralized
federated fine-tuning framework with four interlocking components.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Gauge-invariant heterogeneous aggregation}
        (\S\ref{sec:method-agg}): a gossip operator acting in
        $\dW$-space with per-client SVD re-factorization, well-defined for
        arbitrary and time-varying rank profiles.
  \item \textbf{Capability- and complexity-aware rank allocation}
        (\S\ref{sec:method-alloc}): each client's rank is the quantized
        minimum of a \emph{resource ceiling} (derived from memory, compute,
        and bandwidth budgets) and a \emph{demand signal} (derived from
        local gradient spectra and a domain-complexity score combining
        embedding entropy, diversity, intrinsic dimension, and label
        imbalance).
  \item \textbf{Domain discovery from adapter signatures}
        (\S\ref{sec:method-cluster}): clients are clustered by the
        similarity of their effective updates, requiring no raw data, no
        labels, and no coordinator; clustering is refreshed periodically as
        adapters evolve.
  \item \textbf{Hierarchical gossip with learned cross-domain transfer}
        (\S\ref{sec:method-gossip}): frequent uniform gossip within
        clusters; sparse cross-cluster exchange through representatives,
        weighted by a learned transfer matrix $T$ in which $T_{k\ell}$
        quantifies how much domain $k$'s adapter helps domain $\ell$---the
        system's answer to ``how much should cat recognition influence dog
        recognition,'' learned rather than hand-set.
\end{enumerate}
A fifth, cross-cutting element---\emph{dynamic rank adaptation}
(\S\ref{sec:method-adapt})---exploits the fact that re-factorization makes
changing rank a local $O(1)$-round operation: clients grow or shrink $r_i$
during training in response to convergence stalls, domain drift, and
resource fluctuation.

\paragraph{Research questions.}
Several of our design choices rest on empirical premises that we test rather
than assume. We organize the evaluation around four questions.
\begin{description}[leftmargin=2em, itemsep=2pt]
  \item[RQ1 (Discoverability).] Under what conditions does latent domain
        structure leave a recoverable signature in LoRA adapters? This is
        \emph{not} automatic: with adapters restricted to a single
        classification head, clustering quality degrades with training rather
        than improving (\S\ref{sec:exp-clustering}). We characterize the
        adapter placement, feature representation (factor spectra vs.\
        effective updates), and similarity metric required for reliable
        discovery.
  \item[RQ2 (Selective aggregation).] Does hierarchical, transfer-weighted
        gossip improve per-domain accuracy and fairness over flat,
        domain-blind gossip at equal or lower communication cost?
  \item[RQ3 (Heterogeneous merging).] Does $\dW$-space aggregation preserve
        accuracy under rank heterogeneity where factor-space schemes
        (zero-padding, truncation) degrade?
  \item[RQ4 (Allocation).] Does coupling rank to measured resource ceilings
        \emph{and} data-complexity demand outperform uniform and
        resource-only allocation at matched communication budgets?
\end{description}

\paragraph{Contributions.}
This paper contributes:
(1) the first framework, to our knowledge, for fully decentralized
federated fine-tuning with heterogeneous, dynamically adjustable LoRA ranks
across undeclared domains (\S\ref{sec:method});
(2) a gauge-invariant aggregation primitive for low-rank adapters, with an
analysis of why factor-space averaging is ill-posed
(Proposition~\ref{prop:gauge});
(3) a serverless domain-discovery mechanism based on adapter signatures,
together with an empirical characterization of when it succeeds and fails
(RQ1, \S\ref{sec:exp-clustering});
(4) a learned cross-domain transfer mechanism that turns ``how much should
domain $k$ influence domain $\ell$'' from a hyperparameter into a measured,
evolving quantity (\S\ref{sec:method-gossip});
(5) a convergence analysis of hierarchical, transfer-weighted gossip with
time-varying mixing weights (Theorem~\ref{thm:convergence}); and
(6) a multi-seed empirical study across vision and language multi-domain
benchmarks with ablations of every component (\S\ref{sec:experiments}).

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We organize prior work along the three axes whose intersection defines our
setting; Table~\ref{tab:positioning} summarizes the landscape.

\subsection{Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning and Rank Selection}
LoRA~\citep{hu2022lora} reparameterizes weight updates as low-rank products,
and its variants reduce memory further via
quantization~\citep{dettmers2023qlora}. The choice of rank is recognized as
consequential even in the centralized setting: AdaLoRA~\citep{zhang2023adalora}
prunes singular directions adaptively during training, and
DyLoRA~\citep{valipour2023dylora} trains adapters that are simultaneously
valid at multiple ranks. These methods adapt rank \emph{within one trainer
holding all the data}; none contends with rank choices distributed across
mutually invisible datasets and devices, nor with merging adapters of
different ranks---the problems that define our setting. We do, however,
inherit their central lesson: appropriate rank is a property of the
\emph{task}, not a global constant. Our allocation rule
(\S\ref{sec:method-alloc}) additionally treats the LoRA scaling $\alpha$
jointly with $r$ through the effective scale $\alpha/r$, since the two are
not independently identifiable.

\subsection{Federated Fine-Tuning with LoRA}
A rapidly growing line of work runs LoRA inside server-orchestrated FL:
FedIT~\citep{zhang2023fedit} federates instruction tuning;
SLoRA~\citep{babakniya2023slora} addresses initialization under data
heterogeneity. Closest to us are methods embracing \emph{rank}
heterogeneity: HetLoRA~\citep{cho2024hetlora} lets clients hold different
ranks and reconciles them by zero-padding and self-pruning, while
FlexLoRA~\citep{bai2024flexlora} aggregates full updates at the server and
redistributes client-specific ranks via SVD. FFA-LoRA~\citep{sun2024ffalora}
freezes $A$ and trains only $B$, sidestepping part of the aggregation
nonlinearity at the cost of expressiveness. All of these presuppose a
central server that sees every client's update each round---the server
\emph{is} the aggregation algorithm. Our $\dW$-space operator can be read as
a decentralization of FlexLoRA's server-side SVD: the re-factorization
becomes a local, per-client operation applied to a neighborhood mixture, at
which point the server is no longer load-bearing. Domain structure,
moreover, is absent from all of these methods' aggregation rules.

\subsection{Decentralized and Gossip-Based Learning}
Decentralized SGD with gossip averaging is classical~\citep{boyd2006gossip,
lian2017dpsgd}, with a mature convergence theory parameterized by the
spectral gap of the mixing matrix~\citep{koloskova2020unified} and an
understanding that topology matters for the transient
regime~\citep{neglia2020topology}. Compressed~\citep{koloskova2019choco} and
directed~\citep{assran2019sgp} variants improve practicality. This
literature, however, assumes every node holds a tensor of \emph{the same
shape} and mixes with \emph{data-independent} weights; both assumptions fail
in our setting (C1, C2). We import the analytical machinery---mixing
matrices, spectral gaps---but must re-derive it for cluster-structured,
time-varying, transfer-weighted mixing over heterogeneous-rank adapters.

\subsection{Clustered and Personalized Federated Learning}
Clustered FL acknowledges that one global model cannot serve heterogeneous
clients: CFL~\citep{sattler2020cfl} recursively bipartitions clients by
gradient similarity; IFCA~\citep{ghosh2020ifca} alternates cluster assignment
and per-cluster training; \citet{marfoq2021fedem} model client distributions
as mixtures. Personalization methods such as Ditto~\citep{li2021ditto}
interpolate between local and global objectives and motivate our fairness
metrics. Two gaps separate this literature from our setting. First, the
clustering step is executed \emph{by the server} on globally collected
models or gradients; we must cluster from each node's partial, neighborhood
view. Second, clusters in these methods partition clients into
\emph{isolated} training groups, whereas our learned transfer matrix keeps
clusters porous---cross-domain knowledge flows, but at learned, asymmetric
rates. \todo{Double-check the isolation claim against the IFCA paper's

\paragraph{The gap.} Reading Table~\ref{tab:positioning} column-wise:
serverless methods are rank-homogeneous and domain-blind; rank-heterogeneous
methods are server-bound; domain-aware methods are server-bound \emph{and}
rank-homogeneous. The combination---required simultaneously by realistic
cross-silo deployments---is unoccupied. Occupying it is not a matter of
concatenating existing techniques, because their enabling assumptions
conflict (\S\ref{sec:intro}); it requires an aggregation primitive that is
simultaneously shape-agnostic, similarity-aware, and local.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{System Model}
\label{sec:problem-system}

Let $\clients = \{1, \dots, N\}$ be a set of clients connected by an
undirected communication graph $\graph = (\clients, \edges)$; client $i$ may
exchange messages only with its neighbors $\neigh{i} = \{j : (i,j) \in
\edges\}$. There is no central server, and no entity observes more than its
own neighborhood. \todo{State assumptions on $\graph$: connected, possibly
time-varying? Decide whether we handle churn/dropout in v1.}

Each client holds a private dataset $S_i = \{(x, y)\}$ drawn from a
distribution $P_{d(i)}$, where $d : \clients \to \{1, \dots, K\}$ assigns
clients to one of $K$ \emph{latent domains}. Neither $K$ nor $d$ is known to
any party; domains are an analytical construct that the system may attempt
to estimate, not an input. Within a domain, client datasets are
non-identically distributed samples (e.g., label-skewed shards) of the
domain distribution.

All clients share a frozen pretrained backbone $\theta_0$. Client $i$
maintains, for each adapted layer $l \in \mathcal{L}$, LoRA factors
$A_i^{(l)} \in \R^{r_i \times d^{(l)}_{\mathrm{in}}}$ and $B_i^{(l)} \in
\R^{d^{(l)}_{\mathrm{out}} \times r_i}$ with client-specific rank $r_i$ and
scaling $\alpha_i$, inducing the effective update
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:effective-update}
\dW_i^{(l)} \;=\; \frac{\alpha_i}{r_i}\, B_i^{(l)} A_i^{(l)}
\;\in\; \R^{d^{(l)}_{\mathrm{out}} \times d^{(l)}_{\mathrm{in}}},
\end{equation}
and the personalized model $\theta_i = \theta_0 \oplus \{\dW_i^{(l)}\}_{l
\in \mathcal{L}}$. We suppress the layer index $l$ hereafter; all per-layer
operations apply independently to each adapted layer.

\subsection{Heterogeneity and Resource Model}
\label{sec:problem-resources}

Client $i$ has a resource profile $\rho_i$ (accelerator memory, compute
throughput, link bandwidth) that induces a \emph{rank ceiling}
$R^{\max}_i$: the largest rank for which local training and per-round
communication fit within $i$'s budgets. Ranks are drawn from a quantized
menu $\mathcal{R} = \{r_{\min}, \dots, r_{\max}\}$ (powers of two in our
implementation). The map $\rho_i \mapsto R^{\max}_i$ is specified in
\S\ref{sec:method-alloc}. System heterogeneity means $R^{\max}_i$ varies by
one to two orders of magnitude across clients; any admissible algorithm must
remain executable by the weakest participant.

\subsection{Objective}
\label{sec:problem-objective}

Because domains are genuinely different, a single consensus model is the
wrong target. We seek \emph{personalized} adapters that minimize the average
local risk,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:objective}
\min_{\{(A_i, B_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}}\;
\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N}
\E_{(x,y) \sim P_{d(i)}}\!\left[\loss\big(f(x;\, \theta_0 \oplus \dW_i),\, y\big)\right]
\quad \text{s.t.} \quad
r_i \le R^{\max}_i \;\; \forall i,
\end{equation}
subject to communication occurring only along $\edges$. Collaboration enters
not through the objective---which is separable---but through the algorithm:
clients benefit exactly insofar as neighbors' updates carry transferable
signal. The design problem is therefore \emph{who should average with whom,
and how much}, under the rank and topology constraints. Three quantities
beyond average risk matter for evaluating a solution:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Fairness across domains.} With per-domain accuracy
        $\mathrm{acc}_k$ (the mean over clients in domain $k$), the gap
        $\Gamma = \max_k \mathrm{acc}_k - \min_k \mathrm{acc}_k$ should be
        small: no domain should subsidize the others.
  \item \textbf{Communication.} Total bytes transmitted; for client $i$ a
        LoRA payload costs $\Theta\big(r_i (d_{\mathrm{in}} +
        d_{\mathrm{out}})\big)$ per layer per message, so cost is governed
        jointly by the rank profile, the topology, and the gossip schedule.
  \item \textbf{Participation.} The fraction of clients for which
        collaboration strictly improves over local-only training; a method
        that helps the average by sacrificing the weakest clients is not
        admissible for cross-silo deployment.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Gauge-Invariant Aggregation in Update Space}
\label{sec:method-agg}

The natural extension of FedAvg to LoRA---averaging $A$ and $B$ factors
entry-wise---is ill-posed for two independent reasons.

\begin{proposition}[Factor averaging is not well-defined]
\label{prop:gauge}
Let $(B_1, A_1)$ and $(B_2, A_2)$ be LoRA factor pairs of equal rank $r$.
(i) For any invertible $U \in \R^{r\times r}$, the pair $(B_2 U,\,
U^{-1}A_2)$ represents the same effective update as $(B_2, A_2)$, yet the
factor average $\tfrac12(B_1 + B_2 U)\,\tfrac12(A_1 + U^{-1}A_2)$ varies
with $U$; the average of two fixed updates is therefore not a function of
the updates themselves. (ii) The factor average contains spurious
cross terms: $\tfrac14 (B_1 + B_2)(A_1 + A_2) \neq \tfrac12(B_1 A_1 + B_2
A_2)$ in general.
\todo{Write the two-line proof; (i) by exhibiting $U$, e.g.\ a permutation,
(ii) by expansion. Consider adding a small empirical demo: cosine similarity
of factor-averaged vs.\ $\dW$-averaged updates as $U$ is randomized.}
\end{proposition}

Heterogeneous ranks add a third obstruction: factors of different shapes
cannot be averaged at all, and the standard repairs are lossy in opposite
ways (zero-padding implicitly embeds low-rank clients in the high-rank
client's arbitrary basis; truncation discards the high-rank client's tail).

All three problems dissolve in update space. The effective update
$\dW_i = (\alpha_i / r_i) B_i A_i$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:effective-update} is
invariant to the gauge $U$, has shape $d_{\mathrm{out}} \times
d_{\mathrm{in}}$ independent of $r_i$, and absorbs the per-client scaling
$\alpha_i / r_i$, so clients with different $(\alpha, r)$ conventions
combine correctly without renormalization. We define the neighborhood
aggregation operator at client $i$ with mixing weights $w_{ij}$
(\S\ref{sec:method-gossip}):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dw-agg}
\overline{\dW}_i \;=\; \sum_{j \in \neigh{i} \cup \{i\}} w_{ij}\, \dW_j,
\qquad \sum_{j} w_{ij} = 1,\; w_{ij} \ge 0 .
\end{equation}
The client then returns to its own parameterization by truncated SVD. With
$\overline{\dW}_i = U \Sigma V^{\!\top}$ and $\sigma_1 \ge \sigma_2 \ge
\cdots$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:refactor}
\refact_{r_i}\!\big(\overline{\dW}_i\big):\qquad
B_i \leftarrow U_{:,1:r_i} \Sigma_{1:r_i}^{1/2},
\qquad
A_i \leftarrow \Sigma_{1:r_i}^{1/2} V_{:,1:r_i}^{\!\top},
\end{equation}
(with the $\alpha_i/r_i$ scale factored back out), which is the best
rank-$r_i$ approximation of the aggregate in Frobenius norm by
Eckart--Young. Information loss is thus explicit, optimal for the given
budget, and quantified by the discarded tail $\sum_{m > r_i} \sigma_m^2$,
which we plan to log as a per-round diagnostic.
%% NOTE: logging the discarded spectral mass is cheap and gives us a
%% beautiful ablation figure: how much signal do low-rank clients actually
%% lose? It also empirically supports the rank-allocation story.

Two properties make this operator practical. \emph{Cost:} the aggregate
$\overline{\dW}_i$ has rank at most $\sum_{j \in \neigh{i} \cup \{i\}} r_j
\ll \min(d_{\mathrm{in}}, d_{\mathrm{out}})$, so the SVD can be computed on
a thin matrix (e.g., by orthogonalizing the stacked factors) rather than on
the full $d_{\mathrm{out}} \times d_{\mathrm{in}}$ update.
\todo{Spell out the thin computation and measure overhead.}
\emph{Rank agility:} because every round ends in a re-factorization, a
client may change $r_i$ between rounds at zero additional cost
(\S\ref{sec:method-adapt}), and may even refactor to a rank different from
all of its neighbors'.

\subsection{Capability- and Complexity-Aware Rank Allocation}
\label{sec:method-alloc}

A client's rank should reflect what it \emph{can afford} and what its data
\emph{demands}; the two are independent quantities and existing
heterogeneous-LoRA work conflates them by deriving rank from resources
alone. AH-LoRA computes both and takes the (quantized) minimum:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:allocation}
r_i \;=\; q_{\mathcal{R}}\!\Big( \min\big\{ R^{\max}_i,\; \max\{\, r_{\min},\;
\underbrace{c_i\, R^{\max}_i}_{\text{capability floor}},\;
\underbrace{\min( s_i,\, R^{\max}_i )}_{\text{demand}} \,\} \big\} \Big),
\end{equation}
where $q_{\mathcal{R}}$ rounds to the rank menu.
%% NOTE: Eq form merges P1's rank_equation with P2's complexity pipeline;
%% reconcile exact form with the code before submission (rank_selector.py).

\paragraph{Resource ceiling $R^{\max}_i$.} Derived from the device profile
as the largest menu rank satisfying three budgets: adapter memory
($2 r (d_{\mathrm{in}} {+} d_{\mathrm{out}})$ parameters per layer plus
optimizer state), per-round training time, and per-message payload under the
client's bandwidth. \todo{Final formula + measurement procedure; cf.\
resource profiler implementation.}

\paragraph{Demand signal $s_i$.} Two complementary estimators, both
computable locally and cheaply:
(i) the \emph{gradient stable rank} $s(G) = \norm{G}_F^2 / \norm{G}_2^2$ of
local loss gradients at the adapted layers, a probe of how many directions
the local task is actually pushing on---computed from a handful of
mini-batches before training begins;
(ii) a \emph{domain complexity score} $\Phi_i \in [0,1]$ aggregating
embedding entropy, pairwise diversity, intrinsic dimensionality, baseline
task difficulty, and label imbalance of the local data under the frozen
backbone. \todo{Specify the exact combination of $s(G)$ and $\Phi_i$ into
$s_i$; current implementations use them separately (P1 vs P2) --- unify, and
ablate each.}
The capability floor $c_i \in [0,1]$ (an increasing function of the
client's capability tier) prevents well-resourced clients from
under-allocating purely due to a noisy early demand estimate.

\paragraph{On the scaling $\alpha$.} The pair $(\alpha, r)$ affects the
forward pass only through the product $\frac{\alpha}{r}BA$ and the
optimizer dynamics only through the effective scale $\alpha/r$; $\alpha$ is
therefore not identifiable independently of $r$ and the learning rate, and
``optimal $\alpha$'' findings that ignore this coupling are easily
artifactual (in particular, correlating $r$ with $\alpha/r$ is negatively
biased by construction). AH-LoRA fixes the effective scale $\alpha_i / r_i$
to a constant across clients so that heterogeneous ranks do not silently
induce heterogeneous effective learning rates, and the $\dW$-space operator
absorbs whatever per-client scale remains.

\subsection{Serverless Domain Discovery from Adapter Signatures}
\label{sec:method-cluster}

\subsection{Hierarchical Gossip with Learned Cross-Domain Transfer}
\label{sec:method-gossip}

Given cluster estimates $\widehat{\domain{1}}, \dots,
\widehat{\domain{\widehat{K}}}$, AH-LoRA structures communication in two
tiers.

\paragraph{Within-domain tier.} Clients gossip every round with neighbors
in the same estimated cluster using Eq.~\eqref{eq:dw-agg} with uniform (or
data-size-weighted) mixing weights---peers solving the same problem should
average aggressively. \todo{Specify intra-cluster topology maintenance
(ring over cluster members in v1).}

\paragraph{Cross-domain tier.} Every $\tau_x$ rounds, designated
representatives of each cluster exchange cluster-level adapters. The mixing
weights of this exchange are not uniform but given by a \emph{transfer
matrix} $T \in \R^{\widehat{K} \times \widehat{K}}$, learned from held-out
local validation data: each cluster evaluates every other cluster's
(communicated) adapter on a small local validation split, and
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:transfer}
T_{k\ell} \;=\;
\frac{\exp\!\big(v_\ell(k) / \kappa\big)}
     {\sum_{k'} \exp\!\big(v_\ell(k') / \kappa\big)},
\end{equation}
where $v_\ell(k)$ is the validation utility of cluster $k$'s adapter on
cluster $\ell$'s data and $\kappa$ a temperature. $T_{k\ell}$ is the
system's learned answer to ``how much should domain $k$ influence domain
$\ell$'': asymmetric, data-driven, and re-estimated as training proceeds.
Cross-tier aggregation then applies Eq.~\eqref{eq:dw-agg} at representatives
with weights $w_{\ell k} \propto T_{k\ell}$, followed by intra-cluster
dissemination.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{AH-LoRA (one training round at client $i$; global view shown for
clarity)}
\label{alg:ahlora}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Initialization:} profile resources $\rho_i \!\to\! R^{\max}_i$;
estimate demand $s_i$; set $r_i$ by Eq.~\eqref{eq:allocation}; fix effective
scale $\alpha_i/r_i \equiv \gamma$.
\For{round $t = 1, 2, \dots$}
  \State Local training: $E$ epochs on $S_i$, updating $(A_i, B_i)$ only.
  \If{$t \equiv 0 \pmod{\tau_c}$} \Comment{refresh domain estimate}
     \State exchange signatures; re-cluster; update $\widehat{\domain{}}$,
            representatives.
  \EndIf
  \State \textbf{Within-domain gossip:} $\overline{\dW}_i \gets$
         Eq.~\eqref{eq:dw-agg} over same-cluster neighbors.
  \If{$t \equiv 0 \pmod{\tau_x}$ and $i$ is a representative}
     \State \textbf{Cross-domain exchange:} update $T$ by
            Eq.~\eqref{eq:transfer}; mix cluster adapters with weights
            $\propto T$.
  \EndIf
  \State $(A_i, B_i) \gets \refact_{r_i}(\overline{\dW}_i)$
         \Comment{Eq.~\eqref{eq:refactor}}
  \If{$t \equiv 0 \pmod{\tau_r}$} \Comment{rank adaptation,
      \S\ref{sec:method-adapt}}
     \State update $r_i$ from convergence/resource/drift signals;
            (re-factorization already grants the new shape).
  \EndIf
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Dynamic Rank Adaptation}
\label{sec:method-adapt}

Initial allocation (\S\ref{sec:method-alloc}) is an estimate made before
training; AH-LoRA revises it online. Every $\tau_r$ rounds, client $i$
forms an adjustment decision from three local signals: (i) \emph{relative
convergence} --- its loss-decrease rate compared to that reported by
neighbors (a client converging much slower than its cluster may be
capacity-limited; much faster, over-provisioned); (ii) \emph{resource
headroom} --- current memory/compute availability versus profile; and (iii)
\emph{drift} --- a distribution-shift statistic on recent batches under the
frozen backbone. The decision moves $r_i$ one step on the menu
$\mathcal{R}$, and the $\refact$ step of the very next round realizes the
new rank; no retraining or re-initialization is required. The spectral tail
discarded at refactorization (\S\ref{sec:method-agg}) provides a natural
guardrail: a client repeatedly discarding significant mass is a candidate
for rank increase.

\begin{assumption}[Smoothness, bounded variance, connectivity]
\label{ass:standard}
Each local objective is $L$-smooth; stochastic gradients are unbiased with
variance bounded by $\sigma^2$; gradient heterogeneity is bounded within
clusters ($\zeta_{\mathrm{in}}^2$) and across clusters
($\zeta_{\mathrm{out}}^2$); each cluster's gossip matrix is doubly stochastic
with spectral gap at least $p$; and the per-round refactorization error is
bounded by the discarded tail mass $\epsilon_r$.

\end{assumption}

\begin{theorem}[Convergence of AH-LoRA, informal]
\label{thm:convergence}

clustering reaches an $\epsilon$-stationary point of

spectral-gap dependence in the transient term and an additive bias controlled
by the refactorization tail $\epsilon_r$ and the transfer-mixing error. The
result extends to time-varying ranks under a bounded switch frequency and to
estimated clusters via a perturbation argument on the mixing matrix.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Privacy.} Removing the central server eliminates the all-seeing
aggregator, but exchanged adapters still leak information about local data,
and AH-LoRA makes no formal privacy guarantee. Because all communication is
mediated by the $\dW$-space operator, differentially private noise on the
exchanged updates and secure pairwise aggregation compose with our method
without changing its structure; we leave a quantified privacy--utility study
to future work.

\paragraph{Dependence on discoverability.} The hierarchical tier is only as
good as the domain estimate. When domains do not separate in adapter space
(the RQ1 failure regime), clustering is unreliable and the method should
degrade gracefully toward flat gossip rather than mis-route transfer. \todo{
Quantify this degradation: the cost of mis-clustering as a function of
clustering ARI, and confirm the graceful-degradation claim empirically.}

\paragraph{Allocation: predictor accuracy vs.\ benefit.} Our allocation rule
couples rank to a data-complexity predictor, yet preliminary evidence
suggests the predictor can be weak while heterogeneous allocation still
helps. This points to a subtlety worth stating plainly: the benefit may stem
from rank \emph{diversity} and capability-matching rather than from
accurately forecasting each client's optimal rank. \todo{Resolve with the
RQ4 study that separates the two effects; revise this paragraph once the
multi-seed result is in.}

\paragraph{Cost of learned transfer.} Estimating $T$ requires each cluster to
evaluate others' adapters on held-out local data, consuming validation
samples and representative-side compute on the cross-domain schedule
$\tau_x$; the schedule trades transfer freshness against overhead.

\paragraph{Scope.} AH-LoRA targets cross-silo federation---tens to hundreds
of persistent, stateful clients---rather than cross-device settings with
millions of intermittent participants. The convergence analysis
(\S\ref{sec:theory}) currently covers fixed clusters and ranks; the gap to
the fully adaptive system is bridged only informally. \todo{State which
extensions the final theorem covers rigorously and which remain empirical.}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We introduced AH-LoRA, a framework that brings heterogeneous-rank, domain-aware
LoRA fine-tuning to the fully decentralized setting. Its enabling idea is to
aggregate in the space of effective updates $\dW = (\alpha/r)BA$ rather than
over LoRA factors: this single change sidesteps the gauge ambiguity that makes
factor averaging ill-posed, renders adapters of any rank---and time-varying
ranks---combinable, and does so locally at every peer, with no server. On this
primitive AH-LoRA builds serverless domain discovery, hierarchical gossip with
a learned cross-domain transfer matrix, and online rank adaptation, addressing
domain, system, and decentralization heterogeneity together rather than one at
a time. \todo{Final paragraph: summarize what the experiments establish ---
